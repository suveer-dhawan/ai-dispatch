Chapter One

The Race

Compute demand is outrunning the machines built to power it.

For decades, the semiconductor industry kept a quiet promise: each new generation of chip would do more work for less energy. Between 2015 and 2019, that promise held. Average training compute for frontier AI models rose from roughly 1020 FLOPs to 1022 — a hundredfold increase — while hardware efficiency tracked a comparable upward slope. Engineers at NVIDIA, Google, and AMD were building faster silicon just quickly enough to match what AI labs demanded of it.

Then the compact broke. After 2020, compute demand began multiplying at 5–9× per year, driven by an industry-wide conviction that scale itself was the path to intelligence. Hardware efficiency, constrained by the physics of transistor fabrication, managed only 1–2× annual gains. The gap compounds: a single year of mismatch is recoverable; five consecutive years is a structural deficit. By the time GPT-4 shipped in 2023, the energy required to train a frontier model had grown by three orders of magnitude in just four years — while the chips running those jobs had barely doubled in efficiency.

The 2025 generation made the crisis impossible to ignore. xAI's Grok 4 consumed an estimated 5.0 × 1026 FLOPs during training. OpenAI's GPT-4.5 followed close behind at 3.8 × 1026. These are not incremental steps — they represent a full order-of-magnitude leap beyond anything attempted just twelve months earlier. Hardware cannot save us from this curve. The machines are losing the race.

Chapter Two

The Map

Geography decides the true carbon cost of intelligence.

What If?

Not all electricity is created equal. Every kilowatt-hour drawn from a national grid carries a carbon signature — shaped by that country's mix of coal, gas, nuclear, and renewables. When an AI model trains for weeks across thousands of GPUs, it inherits the carbon intensity of wherever those GPUs are plugged in. The United States, home to OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, and xAI, dominates the globe: over 33,000 estimated tonnes of CO₂ from AI training alone. Yet America's grid averages roughly 407 gCO₂/kWh across the dataset — middling by global standards, neither clean nor catastrophic. Drag the globe to explore.

The danger is emerging elsewhere. The United Arab Emirates — home to the Falcon-180B model — operates on a grid averaging 484 gCO₂/kWh, almost entirely gas-fired. China, training the ERNIE and Wu Dao series, relies on a grid at 596 gCO₂/kWh, heavily dependent on coal. These are not hypothetical risks. As Middle Eastern sovereign wealth funds and Chinese tech giants pour billions into frontier AI, every model trained in these regions carries a carbon multiplier — the same computation, the same intelligence, but with dramatically higher emissions baked into every floating-point operation.

How much does geography really matter? Use the calculator to find out. Select any model from the dataset, then pick a hypothetical training location. The carbon footprint recalculates instantly. Train Grok 4 in France — where nuclear power keeps the grid at just 56 gCO₂/kWh — and its emissions plummet by over 85%. Train the same model in India, where coal pushes the grid to 632 gCO₂/kWh, and emissions jump by over 50%. Or try Sweden, where hydro and nuclear deliver a grid at just 7 gCO₂/kWh — a 98% reduction. The physics of the model doesn't change. Only the politics of the grid.

Chapter Three

The Shift

Multimodal AI rewrites the energy baseline.

A universal law governs the relationship between a model's size and the energy it consumes. A power-law regression across 57 frontier models yields an R² of 0.768 — meaning roughly 77% of the variance in training energy is explained by parameter count alone. The takeaway is blunt: bigger models mathematically guarantee exponentially higher energy bills, regardless of how cleverly they are optimised. No amount of engineering elegance changes the underlying physics.

But scale is only half the story. The real rupture is architectural. When the industry shifted from single-domain Language models to Multimodal systems — models that simultaneously process text, images, audio, and video — the energy baseline didn't simply increase. It jumped regimes. The median Multimodal model requires over 100x the energy of a median Language model. The horizontal lines on this chart mark those medians. Notice how far apart they sit.

Perhaps the starkest way to see it: the least energy-hungry Multimodal model in the dataset (352 MWh) consumes substantially more than the 75th percentile of all Language models. Every orange dot above sits in a region that blue dots almost never reach. This is not incremental growth — it is a new regime of infrastructure demand, routinely requiring tens of thousands of Megawatt-hours per training run.
